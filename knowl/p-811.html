  1. Draw a moderately large and accurate diagram. Too much confusion has been created by small, inaccurately-drawn diagrams.
  2. Start with the angle \(\theta\) that you found in Part (a) of this example. The angle opposite \(\theta_1\) is \(\theta_2\text{.}\)
  3. Next, using the corresponding angles of parallel lines, transfer \(\theta_2\) from the force triangle to triangle \(ABC\) as \(\theta_3\text{.}\)
  4. Finally, find \(d_{\perp}\) using the sine function.
    \begin{align*} \sin \theta_3 \amp = \frac{d_{\perp}}{AC}\\ d_{\perp} \amp = AC\left(\sin \theta_3\right)\\ \amp = 4\left(\sin \ang{56.3}\right)\\ \amp = \ft{3.328} \end{align*}
  5. Finally, compute the moment about \(A\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} M_A \amp=F\ d_{\perp}\\ \amp =\ft{3.328}\left(\lbf{500}\right)\\ \amp = \ftlbf{1664.10}\\ \vec{M}_A \amp =\ftlbf{1664.10}\left(+\khat\right) \end{align*}
    The \(\left(+\khat\right)\) direction of \(\vec{M}_A\) comes from the observation of the right-hand rule, as scalar moment computations are not directional.
