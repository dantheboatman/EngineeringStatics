Subsubsection Cartesian Coordinates

In Statics the most important coordinate system is the Cartesian system, which was named after the French mathematician René Descartes.

In Cartesian coordinates, the three coordinate axes are straight lines named \(x\text{,}\) \(y\) and \(z\text{.}\) In a two dimensional Cartesian coordinates there is no \(z\) direction, and the \(x\)-axis is usually horizontal and points to the right, and the \(y\)-axis points vertically upward. In three dimensions the \(z\) axis is perpendicular to the other two, and so is also perpendicular to the \(xy\) plane. The positive directions of the axes are selected to make them "right handed." A right-handed coordinate system is one where the \(x\)-axis rotated into the \(y\)-axis according to the right-hand rule determines the positive sense for the \(z\)-axis.

The great benefit of orthogonal coordinate system is that once you separate the components of each vector into parts parallel to the coordinate axes, you can treat the vector components as scalar quantities.

You are free to orient your coordinate system in any direction you choose as long as it remains a right-hand coordinate system.

It is usually mathematically advantageous to align one of the coordinate axis with a major feature of the situation.

Right Hand Rule.

The right-hand rule provides a directional relationship between the \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) axes. Additionally, it establishes the +/- sign convention of rotational vectors like torque or angular velocity. We will discuss the directional relationship between axes here, and the rotational vector will be used extensively in Statics, starting in the Moments and Couples chapter.

You can determine the directional relationship between the \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) axes in three different ways as shown below in Figure 2.4 and Figure 2.6.

Option ❶ is \(\overrightarrow{thumb} \times \overrightarrow{index} = \overrightarrow{middle}\text{,}\) or option ❷ is \(\overrightarrow{index} \times \overrightarrow{middle} = \overrightarrow{thumb}\text{.}\)

Start with fingertips sliding along the first vector (\(\vec{x}\)), then the second vector (\(\vec{y}\)) pushes fingers into a curl, and finally, your thumb defines the direction of the final vector (\(\vec{z}\)). This technique is useful for both crossing linear vectors and also determining the rotational vector direction.

Alternate Coordinate Systems.

Sometimes the geometry of the situation is a poor fit for Cartesian Coordinates, and another system which matches the problem geometry better may be selected in order to reduce mathematical difficulty.

In two dimensions the Polar coordinate system is used for systems with rotational symmetry. In polar coordinates, a point is located by its distance from the origin \(r\text{,}\) and an angle measured from a reference direction, \(\theta\text{,}\) \((r,\theta)\) instead of \((x,y)\text{.}\)

In three dimensions Spherical and cylindrical coordinate systems sometimes used and will be discussed in Subsection 2.4.1.
